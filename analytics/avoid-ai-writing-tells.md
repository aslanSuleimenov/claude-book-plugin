# Avoid AI Writing Tells

Patterns that mark prose as AI-generated. Check against every chapter before saving.

---

## 1. The Rule of Three

**What it is:** Listing things in threes: adjectives, actions, observations.
> "She was tired, frustrated, and alone."
> "He picked up the knife, turned it over, and set it back down."

**Why it's a tell:** AI defaults to three because it's a memorized rhetorical pattern. Real prose varies — sometimes one is enough.

**Fix:** Use the number of items the scene actually needs. One sharp detail beats three vague ones.

---

## 2. Emotional Placeholders

**What it is:** Labeling an emotion instead of rendering it.
> "She felt overwhelmed."
> "He was devastated."
> "A wave of sadness washed over her."
> "Emotion welled up inside him."

**Why it's a tell:** The emotion is named, not earned. The reader is told what to feel instead of placed inside the experience.

**Fix:** Find the concrete: what does the body do when overwhelmed? What does the character notice (or fail to notice) when devastated? Replace the label with sensation, action, perception.

---

## 3. Dialogue That Explains Feelings

**What it is:** A character explains their own emotions in dialogue.
> "I'm so angry because you never listen to me."
> "I feel scared when you do that."
> "I love you, and that's why I'm pulling away."

**Why it's a tell:** Real people rarely name their feelings in the moment. Dialogue carries subtext — what's not said is often what matters.

**Fix:** Let the anger show in what the character says, not in their naming of it. "You never listen" is the anger. Remove the emotion label.

---

## 4. Abstract State Over Concrete Image

**What it is:** Describing atmosphere with abstract clichés.
> "The air was thick with tension."
> "Silence stretched between them."
> "Time seemed to slow."
> "Something in her eyes told him everything."
> "The world seemed smaller somehow."

**Why it's a tell:** These phrases are slots, not images. They register as zero.

**Fix:** What is actually in the room? What is the character's body doing? What specific sound is filling the silence? Find the physical detail that makes the abstract state unnecessary.

---

## 5. Sudden / Suddenly Overuse

**What it is:** Using "suddenly" as a transition or emphasis tool more than once per chapter.
> "Suddenly, she heard a noise."
> "Then, suddenly, everything changed."

**Why it's a tell:** Overuse deadens the word. If everything happens suddenly, nothing does.

**Fix:** Use timing and sentence structure to create suddenness. Short sentence. New paragraph. The abruptness IS the sudden.

---

## 6. The Filler Gesture Trio

**What it is:** Nodding, smiling, and sighing as the default character physicality.
> He nodded. / She smiled. / He sighed.

**Why it's a tell:** These are the three gestures AI uses when it knows a character needs to do something but doesn't know what. They carry almost no information.

**Fix:** What is this specific character's physical habit? What does their body do when nervous, pleased, uncertain? Use body language that is character-specific and informative.

---

## 7. Weather as Mood Indicator (Pathetic Fallacy Overuse)

**What it is:** Using weather to signal character emotion or story tone.
> "It was a dark and stormy night as she confronted her past."
> "The sun was shining, but she felt cold inside."

**Why it's a tell:** The mirror relationship between weather and emotion is the oldest trick in the book — so old it's invisible.

**Fix:** Use weather as context, not as emotional annotation. The rain doesn't care about your protagonist. Sometimes sunny weather during tragedy is more devastating than rain.

---

## 8. Rhetorical Questions in Narrative

**What it is:** The narrator (not a character) asks a rhetorical question.
> "What could she do? Nothing."
> "Was this really happening? It was."
> "How had it come to this?"

**Why it's a tell:** Rhetorical questions in narration are a way of marking significance without doing the work of showing why something is significant.

**Fix:** State or show. If it's significant, the scene should make it significant without flagging it.

---

## 9. Protagonist Looks in Mirror

**What it is:** The character looks in a mirror (or water, window, etc.) to deliver a physical description of themselves.

**Why it's a tell:** This is the most notorious exposition-delivery device. Every reader recognizes it.

**Fix:** Weave physical description naturally into scene and action. Another character can notice. The POV character can register their appearance when it's relevant (checking for blood, for a job interview, etc.).

---

## 10. Telling Character Realization

**What it is:** Explicitly narrating that a character has understood something.
> "She realized that she had never truly known him."
> "In that moment, he understood what he had to do."
> "It dawned on her that everything had changed."

**Why it's a tell:** Realization-narrating stops the story to label its own significance.

**Fix:** Show the realization through what the character does next. If she truly never knew him, show us a scene where she acts on that with new eyes. The action IS the realization.

---

## 11. Overly Balanced Sentence Structure

**What it is:** Every sentence following a similar rhythm and length.
> "She walked to the window. She looked outside. The street was empty. She turned away."

**Why it's a tell:** Uniform sentence rhythm is the signature of text generation. Real prose varies.

**Fix:** Vary sentence length deliberately. Short sentences for tension and impact. Longer sentences when you need to build texture, accumulate detail, carry the reader through a sustained moment without interruption. Mix them.

---

## 12. As If / As Though Overuse

**What it is:** Excessive use of "as if" comparisons.
> "She moved as if she were made of glass."
> "He looked at her as if seeing her for the first time."
> "The silence felt as though it might shatter."

**Why it's a tell:** "As if" is a soft comparison — evocative but imprecise. AI uses it frequently to generate imagery without committing.

**Fix:** Use direct metaphor or direct image. Or cut the comparison entirely if the plain verb is stronger.

---

## Summary Checklist

Before saving a chapter, check:
- [ ] No list of three items where one would do
- [ ] No emotion labels — every feeling rendered in body/action/perception
- [ ] No dialogue that names the speaker's own emotion
- [ ] No abstract atmosphere clichés
- [ ] "Suddenly" used at most once, for genuine effect
- [ ] No default nod/smile/sigh without character-specific physicality
- [ ] Weather is context, not mood indicator
- [ ] No mirror scene
- [ ] No "she realized" / "it dawned on him"
- [ ] Sentence rhythm varies — mix of short and long
- [ ] "As if" used sparingly, with intent
