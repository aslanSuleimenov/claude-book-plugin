# Genre Mechanics — Fiction

Structural contracts for fiction genres. Each entry: Promise / Must happen / Forbidden.

---

## Literary Fiction

**Promise:** Emotional truth and a changed understanding of the human condition. The reader will feel something real and be made to think.

**Must happen:**
- A character undergoes genuine internal change (not just external change)
- Language does work beyond conveying information — it creates texture and meaning
- Ambiguity is honored: the story doesn't resolve into easy answers
- The ordinary is rendered strange or significant

**Forbidden:**
- Plot resolving all problems neatly
- Theme stated explicitly by a character
- Characters as mouthpieces for positions
- Prose that could be skimmed without loss

---

## Thriller

**Promise:** Escalating tension with a protagonist in danger who must act to survive/prevent catastrophe. The reader should feel urgency and fear.

**Must happen:**
- Ticking clock or escalating stakes
- The protagonist must be in genuine jeopardy
- A worthy antagonist with coherent motivation
- A climax where protagonist and antagonist directly confront
- Resolution that answers the central threat

**Forbidden:**
- Protagonist rescued by coincidence or outside help (not their own agency)
- The threat turning out to be fake or easily dismissed
- Too many chapters with no increase in danger
- Antagonist who is evil without explanation

---

## Mystery

**Promise:** A puzzle that will be solved. The reader should feel the satisfaction of "of course!" at the reveal.

**Must happen:**
- A crime or central mystery introduced early
- Clues visible to the reader (fair play)
- Red herrings that mislead without cheating
- A revelation that recontextualizes everything
- Resolution that answers the mystery

**Forbidden:**
- Clues withheld from the reader that the detective had
- A killer who wasn't introduced before the reveal
- Solution that depends on information not in the text
- Coincidence solving the mystery (not detective agency)

---

## Romance

**Promise:** Two people will fall in love and end up together. The emotional journey is the point.

**Must happen:**
- The couple meets and there is chemistry (even if as antagonism)
- An internal obstacle (not just external) keeping them apart
- Genuine emotional vulnerability from both parties
- A dark moment where the relationship seems lost
- A happy ending or happy-for-now (HEA/HFN) — non-negotiable

**Forbidden:**
- No HEA/HFN ending
- A third party "love interest" who has a better claim than the protagonist's partner
- The central obstacle being a simple misunderstanding easily resolved by conversation
- One party behaving in ways that make the relationship feel unhealthy without acknowledgment

---

## Fantasy

**Promise:** Entry into a world with its own rules, where magic and myth make the impossible possible. Wonder, danger, and stakes that feel real.

**Must happen:**
- The world's rules established and honored (magic systems, physical laws)
- The protagonist undergoes a quest or transformation
- The world's stakes are clear — what happens if the protagonist fails?
- The climax uses elements established in the world (Chekhov's magic)

**Forbidden:**
- The rules of the world violated for plot convenience
- "It was all a dream" or equivalent
- Power escalation with no cost
- A chosen one prophecy without complication or subversion

---

## Science Fiction

**Promise:** A "what if" extrapolated seriously. The idea is the engine; the human story is the test case.

**Must happen:**
- The central speculative premise is established and taken seriously
- The idea has real consequences for characters and world
- The story explores something true about current humanity through the speculative lens
- The speculation is internally consistent

**Forbidden:**
- Technobabble that contradicts established rules to solve the plot
- The speculative premise abandoned mid-story
- Science as magic with no rules
- Characters who don't react like people would to the speculative reality

---

## Horror

**Promise:** Fear. A threat the protagonist cannot easily overcome, that violates safety.

**Must happen:**
- Atmosphere established early (dread before the monster)
- The threat is real and has real consequences
- Vulnerability — the protagonist cannot simply fight their way out
- A confrontation with the fear source

**Forbidden:**
- The monster fully explained (mystery is part of horror)
- All characters surviving without cost
- The threat resolved too easily
- Comedy that fully deflates the tension

---

## Historical Fiction

**Promise:** A living past. The reader should feel transported to another time, with the specificity that makes it real.

**Must happen:**
- Period detail that is accurate and specific (not generic "olden times")
- Characters who think and feel as people of that time would
- Historical events used as story engine, not wallpaper
- A story that could only happen in this time and place

**Forbidden:**
- Modern sensibilities anachronistically inserted without dramatic purpose
- Historical research worn on sleeve (lecture-mode)
- Protagonist who thinks and feels exactly like a 21st-century person
- Errors of fact that a brief check would catch
