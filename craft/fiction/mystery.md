# Mystery — Craft Notes

## What this genre does
Delivers the satisfaction of a puzzle solved. The reader is a detective — they should be able to solve the mystery if they're smart enough, but typically they're not. The reveal should feel both surprising and inevitable.

## Reader contract
A crime or mystery will be solved by the end. The reader will be given fair access to clues. The solution will be satisfying — it won't feel cheated or arbitrary.

## Structure models

**Classic detective:** Crime introduced → investigation → red herrings → revelation → explanation
**Inverted mystery:** Reader knows who did it from the start; tension is whether the detective will catch them (Columbo model)
**Procedural:** Investigation as workplace story; the mystery is the B-plot
**Cozy:** Low violence, amateur sleuth, community setting — atmosphere and character over thriller tension
**Noir:** Cynical detective, corrupt world, the mystery reveals systemic rot — often doesn't fully resolve

## The fair play rule
Every clue the detective uses to solve the case must be visible to the reader. You can hide it (bury in description, presented without emphasis), but you cannot withhold it. If a reader re-reads after the reveal and thinks "that was there all along!" — you've done it right. If they think "I couldn't have known that" — you've broken the contract.

## Voice
First-person detective voice is traditional — it creates intimacy and limits information (reader knows only what detective knows). Third-limited serves the same function. Third-omniscient rarely works because it creates pressure to share what you're hiding.

## Scene-level
Investigation scenes need momentum. Each scene should:
- Reveal a new clue or piece of information
- Eliminate one suspect or establish a new one
- Deepen our understanding of the victim/crime
- Not answer the central question yet

## Reference books
- *The Name of the Rose* (Eco) — atmosphere as mystery, ideas as plot
- *In the Woods* (French) — character over plot, two mysteries
- *Rebecca* (du Maurier) — psychological mystery, unreliable narrator
- *The Long Goodbye* (Chandler) — Marlowe's voice IS the genre
- *And Then There Were None* (Christie) — structure as trap
- *The Seven Deaths of Evelyn Hardcastle* (Turton) — structural invention

## Common mistakes
- The killer is someone introduced in the last 20%
- The clue that solves it is withheld from the reader
- Red herrings that are too obviously red herrings
- The detective explains everything but shows no process
- Too many suspects indistinguishable from each other
