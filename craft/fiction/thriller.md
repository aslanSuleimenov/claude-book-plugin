# Thriller — Craft Notes

## What this genre does
Creates and sustains urgency. The reader must turn the page because the stakes are real and the danger is immediate. Thrillers work through tension — the gap between what the reader fears might happen and what actually happens.

## Reader contract
Something terrible is happening or about to happen. The protagonist is in genuine danger and must act. The reader will be given enough to worry and enough hope to keep going. The ending will resolve the central threat.

## Structure models

**Three-act threat escalation:**
- Act 1: establish world + protagonist, introduce the threat
- Midpoint: the threat becomes personal / the stakes double
- Act 2: escalating obstacles, every solution creates new problems
- Crisis: protagonist stripped of resources, allies, hope
- Climax: protagonist acts from their own agency to defeat the threat
- Resolution: consequences land

**Double timeline thriller:** "It happened" frame + "how it happened" investigation.

**The ticking clock:** A deadline (real or implied) drives every chapter.

## Voice
Thrillers often use close third or first person — we need to be in the protagonist's fear. Short sentences at high-tension moments. Scene-level pacing is everything.

Prose should not show off. Clarity + momentum. Sentence complexity can vary: long sentences in setup, short at moments of danger.

## Scene-level
Every scene increases danger or threatens what the protagonist values. Ask of each scene: what does the protagonist want? What blocks them? What's the cost of failure here?

Even rest scenes should have threat under the surface. The thriller reader should never fully relax.

## Reference books
- *Gone Girl* (Flynn) — unreliable narrators, structure as misdirection
- *The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo* (Larsson) — procedural + character
- *The Silence of the Lambs* (Harris) — relationship as engine of tension
- *No Country for Old Men* (McCarthy) — threat without resolution, violence as idea
- *The Secret History* (Tartt) — inverted mystery, dread from the first page

## Common mistakes
- The protagonist is passive — things happen to them
- Too many chapters at the same tension level (plateau)
- The antagonist's plan doesn't hold up to scrutiny
- Coincidence saves the protagonist
- The threat is resolved before the climax (false ending), then re-introduced
- Too many characters introduced too fast
