# Literary Fiction — Craft Notes

## What this genre does
Renders the inner life with precision. The external plot is a vehicle for psychological and moral truth. Language is load-bearing — not decoration.

## Reader contract
The reader accepts slow plot in exchange for depth of feeling and insight. They want to recognize something true about being human. They will tolerate — even enjoy — ambiguity and lack of resolution if the prose is alive.

## Structure models

**Character arc as structure:** The external events mirror an internal shift. The story ends when the character has changed (or failed to change, which is also an ending).

**Episodic structure:** Linked scenes without a thriller plot. Held together by theme, voice, and accumulation of meaning.

**Dual timeline:** Present-tense story and past-tense backstory intercut, each illuminating the other.

**Single consciousness:** One mind rendering experience — Woolf, Sebald, Knausgård. Structure comes from perception, not event.

## Voice
Literary fiction often has a distinctive, idiosyncratic narrative voice. The voice IS the story in a way that genre fiction typically isn't.

- Narrative distance can vary within a book (close third, distant third, first)
- Free indirect discourse is common and powerful
- The narrator can be unreliable, fragmented, or overtly literary
- Sentence rhythm carries emotional weight — variations in length are intentional

## Scene-level
Literary scenes often resist the goal/conflict/outcome template. Instead:
- A scene might be a moment of perception
- A conversation that is really about something other than what's said
- A memory triggered by the present
- A description that accumulates meaning

That said: even literary fiction needs forward motion. Scenes should change something — the reader's understanding, the character's situation, the emotional register.

## Reference books
- *To the Lighthouse* (Woolf) — consciousness as structure, time as subject
- *Housekeeping* (Robinson) — lyric voice, loss as accumulation
- *A Visit from the Goon Squad* (Egan) — structural experiment, time and music
- *Normal People* (Rooney) — restraint, dialogue doing everything
- *The Remains of the Day* (Ishiguro) — unreliable narrator, repression as form
- *Outline* trilogy (Cusk) — radical interiority through apparent exteriority

## Common mistakes
- Substituting beautiful sentences for forward motion
- Ambiguity as an excuse for not knowing what the story is about
- "Nothing happens" — literary doesn't mean plotless, it means plot-secondary
- Interior monologue that explains rather than enacts
- Mistaking difficulty for depth
