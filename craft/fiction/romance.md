# Romance — Craft Notes

## What this genre does
Traces the emotional journey of two people falling in love. The external plot exists to create and sustain the obstacles to that love. The reader wants to feel the attraction, the tension, and the surrender.

## Reader contract
The central couple will end up together (HEA — happily ever after) or in a committed, hopeful relationship (HFN — happy for now). This is non-negotiable. The reader also expects emotional honesty: the attraction must feel real, the obstacles must feel real, and the resolution must be earned.

## Structure models

**External obstacle:** Society, circumstance, family, enemies prevent the couple from being together
**Internal obstacle (more powerful):** Fear of vulnerability, self-belief, past wound prevents emotional commitment — even when external circumstances allow it
**Enemies-to-lovers:** Antagonism as suppressed attraction; the arc from opposition to surrender
**Friends-to-lovers:** Established trust + the terror of risking it
**Second-chance romance:** They had it, lost it, can they get it back?

The most resonant romances have BOTH an external and internal obstacle — and the external one forces the internal one to be confronted.

## Voice
Close third or first person — we need to be inside the attraction. The POV character should notice everything about the love interest: their body language, speech patterns, contradictions. The reader should feel the pull.

Alternating POV (dual first or dual third) is increasingly standard in romance — it lets the reader see both sides of the tension.

## Scene-level
**Scenes that work:**
- First meeting / first impression (chemistry or antagonism or both)
- Forced proximity / cooperation despite resistance
- Moment of vulnerability where the guard drops
- The almost-kiss or interrupted moment
- The dark night where the relationship seems destroyed
- The choice where the character puts love above their protective self-interest

**The dark moment:** Both parties must believe the relationship is over. Not just "there's an obstacle" but "this cannot happen." The reader should feel genuine loss before the resolution.

## Reference books
- *Pride and Prejudice* (Austen) — enemies-to-lovers, internal obstacle as structure
- *Outlander* (Gabaldon) — external stakes + erotic charge + historical texture
- *The Hating Game* (Thorne) — workplace enemies-to-lovers, voice-driven
- *Beach Read* (Henry) — contemporary, dual POV, humor + vulnerability
- *It Ends with Us* (Hoover) — dark romance, reality of relationship patterns

## Common mistakes
- The "dark moment" is a misunderstanding that a conversation would solve — and the characters don't have that conversation
- The love interest behaves in ways that would be red flags in real life, treated as romantic
- Secondary characters exist only to support the romance and have no lives of their own
- The "HEA" comes too easily after the dark moment
- Chemistry told ("she felt drawn to him") instead of enacted (specific sensory detail of attraction)
