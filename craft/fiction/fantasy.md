# Fantasy — Craft Notes

## What this genre does
Delivers wonder — the experience of a world with different rules. Magic, myth, and the impossible made real. Fantasy holds the capacity for allegory, for rendering moral questions at epic scale, and for the purest forms of adventure.

## Reader contract
The world has its own rules, and those rules will be honored. The stakes are real. The protagonist will be tested. There will be wonder, danger, and a journey — internal and/or external.

## Structure models

**Quest structure:** Protagonist must retrieve/destroy/protect something. The journey is the story.
**Coming-of-age:** Young protagonist discovers power and responsibility (the dominant structure for YA fantasy)
**Political/court fantasy:** Power structures, alliances, betrayal — the magic is the context, the politics are the plot
**Epic multi-POV:** Multiple storylines in a large world, converging at climax (GRRM model)
**Grimdark:** Fantasy that subverts idealism — moral ambiguity, cost of violence, corrupt institutions

## World-building principles
- Establish the rules early, clearly enough that violations feel wrong
- Every rule should have consequences — magic always costs something
- Culture, history, and ecology should feel interconnected (not arbitrary)
- The world should exist beyond the edges of the story
- Info-dump is the primary failure mode — embed world in action and dialogue

**Sanderson's First Law:** The ability to solve problems with magic should be proportional to how well the reader understands that magic.

## Voice
Fantasy can support more narrative distance than contemporary fiction — the voice often has a slightly epic or elevated register. But character interiority still matters. The risk is that the world becomes more interesting than the people.

## Scene-level
Fantasy scenes carry the additional burden of world-building. Every scene should:
- Advance plot or character
- AND establish something about the world

The "rule of two" for exposition: any world-building information should serve at least two purposes (establish the world AND create character OR advance plot).

## Reference books
- *The Name of the Wind* (Rothfuss) — first-person voice, magic system with rules, tragedy seeded from page 1
- *Jonathan Strange & Mr Norrell* (Clarke) — historical register, footnotes as world-building, restraint
- *The Left Hand of Darkness* (Le Guin) — ideas as engine, gender as lens
- *The Way of Kings* (Sanderson) — hard magic systems, massive world-building
- *Piranesi* (Clarke) — small cast, enormous world, wonder as plot

## Common mistakes
- The chosen one prophecy with no complication or cost
- Magic that solves every problem (the rules aren't rules)
- World-building monologue disguised as dialogue ("As you know, Bob...")
- No personal stakes — the hero fights for the world but we don't know what they personally love
- Every problem solved by leveling up
