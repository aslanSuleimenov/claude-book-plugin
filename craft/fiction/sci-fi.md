# Science Fiction — Craft Notes

## What this genre does
Takes a "what if" premise — a scientific or technological or social extrapolation — and follows it seriously. SF uses the speculative to illuminate the present: who we are, what we fear, what we might become.

## Reader contract
The speculative premise will be taken seriously and developed with internal consistency. The story will use the premise to explore something about humanity. The world-building will feel earned and thought-through.

## Structure models

**Novum-driven:** A single speculative idea (the "novum") extrapolated fully. The story is what happens when you take it seriously.
**First contact:** Humanity encounters the genuinely alien — about who we are when faced with radical otherness
**Dystopia/utopia:** Social extrapolation — what if this tendency of ours were taken to its conclusion?
**Space opera:** Epic scale, political/military conflict, multiple civilizations — SF as adventure
**Hard SF:** The science is the point; accuracy matters; ideas drive character
**Soft SF / social SF:** Science as background; culture, language, gender, society as subject (Le Guin, Butler)

## The speculative idea
The idea should be specific enough to generate real constraints and real plot. "What if AI existed" is not a speculative idea — it's too vague. "What if AI was legally a person in a custody dispute" generates real plot.

The idea has implications. Good SF follows the implications rather than using the idea as decoration.

## Voice
SF can support narrative distance and slightly elevated register — you're rendering an unfamiliar world. But the best SF grounds us in a human consciousness experiencing that world.

The risk of SF exposition is high: the author knows how everything works and wants to explain. Resist. Embed world-building in POV and action.

## Scene-level
SF scenes should make the speculative world feel lived-in and real, not explained. Character treats their world as normal — the reader infers the strangeness.

Each scene still needs a goal, conflict, outcome. The SF elements are context and consequence — not substitutes for drama.

## Reference books
- *The Left Hand of Darkness* (Le Guin) — gender as idea, anthropological gaze, prose as argument
- *Blindsight* (Watts) — consciousness, hard neuroscience, bleak first contact
- *The Dispossessed* (Le Guin) — anarchism as experiment, parallel worlds as argument
- *Story of Your Life* (Ted Chiang) — short form, ideas with full emotional weight
- *Children of Time* (Tchaikovsky) — alien cognition as structure, genuinely alien aliens
- *Station Eleven* (Mandel) — post-apocalyptic, literary register, non-linear

## Common mistakes
- The speculative premise abandoned midway through the book
- Technology as magic — no rules, no cost, no consistency
- Characters who don't react as people would to their world
- Explaining the world through character dialogue ("as you know, in the year 2387...")
- SF ideas as metaphor only — no real engagement with the science
- Human psychology unchanged by radically different circumstances
