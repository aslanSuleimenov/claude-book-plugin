# Genre Mechanics — Nonfiction

Structural contracts for nonfiction genres. Each entry: Promise / Must happen / Forbidden.

---

## Memoir

**Promise:** A true story of personal experience, honestly rendered, that illuminates something universal. The reader will see themselves in someone else's life.

**Must happen:**
- The narrator must change or fail to change in a way that matters
- Scene-level specificity — not summary but lived moments
- The narrator must not know what they know at the end when the story begins (the past self must be genuinely past)
- Honesty about the narrator's own flaws and failures
- The universal in the specific — individual experience connected to something larger

**Forbidden:**
- Score-settling presented as truth-telling
- The narrator as perfect victim or perfect hero
- Withholding information the reader needs to understand motivation
- Telescoping years without scene-level grounding
- Claiming certainty about others' inner states you couldn't have known

---

## Narrative Nonfiction

**Promise:** A true story told with the techniques of fiction — scene, character, dialogue, tension. Reality as compelling as a novel.

**Must happen:**
- Real scenes reconstructed from reporting, not summarized from documents
- Characters who feel like people, not sources
- A narrative arc — rising action, climax, consequence
- The reader must never be uncertain what is documented vs. inferred
- The writer's presence clearly positioned (embedded or invisible — choose one)

**Forbidden:**
- Invented dialogue presented as real dialogue
- Composite characters without disclosure
- Scenes that couldn't have been witnessed or reconstructed from records
- The story's meaning stated by the author rather than enacted by the story

---

## Essay

**Promise:** A mind at work on a subject. Not a definitive answer but a genuine inquiry. The reader will think something they hadn't thought before.

**Must happen:**
- A specific inquiry — not "I will discuss X" but "I am trying to understand X"
- Movement — the essay ends somewhere different from where it starts
- The writer's actual uncertainty honored
- The specific detail that makes the abstract concrete

**Forbidden:**
- Thesis-evidence-conclusion structure that forecloses genuine inquiry
- Certainty masquerading as exploration
- The personal as confession rather than lens
- Conclusions that are larger than the inquiry supports

---

## Biography

**Promise:** A full life, or a significant period of a life, rendered with the complexity of a human being. The reader will understand someone — not just their achievements.

**Must happen:**
- The subject as a person, not a hero or villain
- Contradictions honored, not explained away
- The context that shaped the subject (history, culture, family)
- Primary sources — not just received mythology about the subject
- A clear organizing principle (not just chronology)

**Forbidden:**
- Hagiography — uncritical celebration
- Hatchet job — selective use of evidence to condemn
- Projecting psychology the evidence doesn't support
- Missing the gap between what the subject said and what they did

---

## Self-Help / Prescriptive Nonfiction

**Promise:** A method or perspective that will help the reader do or be something better. The reader will leave with actionable insight.

**Must happen:**
- A clear central idea that can be stated simply
- Evidence that the idea is true (research, stories, examples)
- Actionable guidance — not just what to do but how
- Acknowledgment of when the idea doesn't apply

**Forbidden:**
- The core idea padded with repetition to reach book length
- Anecdotes presented as proof of statistical claims
- The idea applies to everyone in all situations
- The author's personal success as the primary evidence

---

## Reportage / Long-form Journalism

**Promise:** This happened. Here is what it means. The reader will understand something about the world they didn't understand before.

**Must happen:**
- Original reporting — not just synthesis of existing sources
- The sources named and verified (or clearly explained why not)
- The stakes stated — why does this matter?
- Fairness to all sides of a dispute

**Forbidden:**
- Anonymous sources for information that should be on record
- Conclusions that outrun the evidence
- The reporter's opinion presented as fact
- False equivalence — treating unequal claims as equal
