# Memoir — Craft Notes

## What this genre does
Renders personal experience with the techniques of fiction. The narrator is simultaneously the person who lived it and the person who survived it and now understands it differently. Memoir is not a diary — it's a shaped narrative about a life, not a life.

## Reader contract
The reader accepts a first-person narrator who will be honest, including about themselves. They want to see something universal in the particular. They will forgive slow parts if the voice is alive. They will not forgive dishonesty or self-pity without self-awareness.

## The double narrator

Memoir lives in the gap between two selves:
- **Then-self:** The person who lived the events, with their ignorance, fears, and misapprehensions intact
- **Now-self:** The narrator writing from a distance, with understanding the then-self didn't have

The trick is to render the then-self's experience authentically while allowing the now-self's understanding to shape the narrative. The now-self should NOT explain the then-self too much — the reader needs to live the confusion and then arrive at understanding alongside the narrator.

## Structure models

**Chronological with scenes:** The dominant mode. Move through time, but scene-level not summary.
**Thematic/braided:** Not chronological — organized by subject, recurring images, or emotional theme. Multiple timelines intercut.
**Lyric memoir:** Closer to essay. Fragmented, associative, prioritizes texture over arc.
**The frame:** The writing of the memoir IS the story — narrator looks back.

## Scene-level
Memoir scenes should feel as specific and lived as fiction scenes:
- Sensory detail from memory (what you actually remember, including what you remember wrong)
- Dialogue reconstructed as accurately as possible, marked as reconstruction
- The emotional experience of the then-self, not yet interpreted
- The scene's place in the larger arc

What memoir scenes should NOT do:
- Summarize where a scene would work ("That year was hard.")
- Explain the scene's meaning immediately after it ("I didn't know then that...")
- Invent details to fill gaps — note the gap honestly

## Voice
Memoir voice is the most personal form of nonfiction voice. It should sound like a specific human being, not a writing style. The reader should feel they know the narrator by the end.

Strong memoir voices have:
- Specific vocabulary and rhythm unique to the narrator
- Consistent attitude toward the past (rueful, angry, grateful, bewildered)
- Moments of uncertainty — "I'm not sure why I did this" is more credible than "I did this because"

## Reference books
- *The Liars' Club* (Karr) — voice and scene, poverty and family
- *The Year of Magical Thinking* (Didion) — grief, the mind at extremity
- *Know My Name* (Miller) — survivor's narrative, institutional critique, voice
- *Between the World and Me* (Coates) — essay-memoir hybrid, address to a son
- *The Glass Castle* (Walls) — restraint, no editorializing, the scene does the work
- *Men We Reaped* (Ward) — braided structure, race and loss

## Common mistakes
- Therapy memoir: "This bad thing happened to me, and I felt bad, and then I healed." No dramatic shape.
- All summary, no scene: telling the reader about experience instead of taking them into it
- The narrator never wrong or complicit in their own problems
- Protecting people too much (the book feels like it's hiding something)
- Backstory dump before the story has earned the reader's investment
