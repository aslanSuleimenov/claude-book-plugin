# Narrative Nonfiction — Craft Notes

## What this genre does
Uses the full toolkit of fiction — scene, character, dialogue, tension, arc — to tell true stories. The goal is a reading experience that feels like a novel but is verifiably real. The power comes from this combination: it's as compelling as fiction AND it actually happened.

## Reader contract
Everything in the book is true (or clearly marked as reconstruction). The techniques of fiction serve the truth, not replace it. The reader will trust the author's reporting and judgment.

## The sourcing question
Everything scene-level must be grounded in documented evidence:
- Interviews with participants
- Documents (diaries, letters, records, transcripts)
- Your own observation
- Established historical record

Where you're reconstructing, mark it: "He must have known..." / "By her account..." / "The records suggest..."

Never: invent composite scenes, attribute thoughts not verified by source, create dialogue from nothing.

## Structure models

**Protagonist-driven:** Follow a single person through an event or period. Gives narrative cohesion at the cost of narrowing.
**Multi-protagonist:** Multiple characters whose stories converge. More complex but can show an event from several angles.
**Procedural/investigation:** The research IS the story — the author discovers as the reader discovers.
**Event-centered:** An event (disaster, trial, crisis) as the organizing spine; multiple characters orbit it.

## Voice
Narrative nonfiction voice is usually more restrained than memoir — the author is present but the story isn't about them. The voice serves the material, not the other way around.

Two modes:
- **Embedded narrator:** The author appears as a character — they're reporting from the field, their presence is part of the story (Herr's *Dispatches*, Orlean's *The Orchid Thief*)
- **Invisible narrator:** The author's presence is minimized — you feel the reporting but not the reporter (Capote's *In Cold Blood*, Larson's *The Devil in the White City*)

## Scene-level
The scene is the atom of narrative nonfiction. A scene requires:
- Specific setting (where, when)
- People doing things (not "sources")
- Reconstructed or recorded dialogue — marked as such
- Sensory specificity that only comes from reporting

The test: if you couldn't have been there and you haven't documented how you know it, you shouldn't write it as scene.

## Research and texture
The specific detail that nobody makes up:
- The brand of cigarette someone was smoking
- The smell of the building
- The exact words of the argument from court records

These details signal truth. Find them. Use them.

## Reference books
- *In Cold Blood* (Capote) — invented the form, invisible narrator, scene from day one
- *The Executioner's Song* (Mailer) — documentary technique, multiple sources
- *The Devil in the White City* (Larson) — parallel structure, historical research as texture
- *Columbine* (Cullen) — myth-correction, deep reporting, careful sourcing
- *Say Nothing* (Radden Keefe) — Northern Ireland, multi-generational, scene-driven
- *I'll Be Gone in the Dark* (McNamara) — investigation as memoir, unfinished arc

## Common mistakes
- "Novelizing" — inventing scenes to fill gaps in the reporting
- Dialogue presented as exact when it's reconstructed
- The reporter's opinions dominating the story
- Sources as functions, not people
- Skipping over the evidence to tell the reader what to think
- False certainty about motivation ("He wanted to be famous" — how do you know?)
